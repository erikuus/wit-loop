Can AI write a witty ending?

This is a small experiment with one unfinished sentence. The question is whether an AI loop can produce an ending that people would recognize as wit, not just as a joke-shaped phrase.

Given beginningi’m lonely, please ...

The Criteria

The rubric in criteria.md treats wit as a sharp turn that is exact, compressed, memorable, fresh, truthful, and graceful. Each item is scored from 0 to 5, then weighted into a final score out of 100.

Reversal and logic

The ending should turn the opening in a way that is surprising but still clean.

Precision

It should feel as if no word can be replaced without making the line less exact.

Compression

The line should do a lot with very few words.

Quotable shape

The full sentence should sound finished and memorable.

Freshness

The idea should feel new without looking desperate to be clever.

Human truth

The joke should reveal something real about people, not just juggle words.

Double tone

The line should feel funny and sad at once, playful on the surface and deeper underneath.

Elegance

The line should move lightly rather than feeling clumsy or overbuilt.

The Loop

The loop is the heart of the experiment. If one conversation tried to invent, judge, and choose all at once, the model could easily flatter its own taste or drift into fuzzy self-justification. Here those jobs are split apart on purpose, so each step knows only what it truly needs.

  1. Creator

    Sees only the sentence beginning. It does not see the rubric, earlier attempts, or the current winner.

  2. Judge

    Sees one completed sentence and the criteria. It does not know what came before or what score it must beat.

  3. Finalizer

    Deterministic code recalculates the score, compares it with the hidden incumbent, and updates the winner only if the number is higher.

What AI Chose

So, after 139 scored attempts, the loop thought its best creation was:

i’m lonely, please stop calling my two-person table 'optimistic seating.'

The judge scored it 81/100. That is strong, but it is not the same thing as canonical wit.

The Original

Melissa Broder's original sentence is much better:

I am lonely, please leave me alone.

It is short, exact, and almost perfectly mirrored: the plea for company immediately becomes a demand for distance.

The turn is funny because it exposes a contradiction. It can sound like a joke about fashionable loneliness, and at the same time like a serious portrait of how people protect themselves from intimacy, anxiety, loss, and grief.

That is the standard the experiment is testing against: wit that is tight, humorous, sad, and deeper than it first appears.
